Five High-Leverage Shifts for Ohio's Digital Learning Standards

These five shifts bring the Ohio Digital Learning Standards (aligned with ISTE) to life in everyday classroom practice. Each shift includes simple moves you can embed tomorrow, repeatable routines, and ideas for larger projects or global collaborations.

By default, all five shifts appear below. Use the pills to focus on one shift at a time, or click "Show All" to see everything again.

View shifts:
Shift 1 – Inquiry to Impact
Big Idea:

Students engage in meaningful inquiry that incorporates multiple perspectives and leads to products or actions that matter to real audiences.

Small / Embedded Moves
  • Ask: "What's the real question here?"
  • Ask: "Whose voice or perspective is missing?"
  • Ask: "Who needs this information and why?"
  • Use a quick "perspective pulse":
    • Represented voice
    • Missing voice
    • Impacted voice
Classroom Routines

Inquiry → Impact Mini-Cycle

  1. Wonder: Generate deeper questions.
  2. Find Out: Curate sources, data, and viewpoints.
  3. Perspective Check: Identify missing voices and biases.
  4. Create for Someone: Make something useful, informative, or influential.
  5. Share → Reflect: Consider audience response and next steps.

Inquiry Walls / Wonder Boards: Students continuously post questions, hypotheses, or interesting patterns.

Mini "Source Set" Reviews: Students evaluate whose perspectives appear and whose are erased.

Expanded / Extended Approaches
  • Cross-class investigations: Two classes explore the same issue from different contexts and co-construct a shared understanding.
  • Real audience projects:
    • Infographics for the school board
    • Local issue explainers for families
    • "Two viewpoints" videos for a partner class in another state
  • Global Café–style collaboration:
    • Synchronous kick-off
    • Asynchronous joint research
    • Final synchronous share

Why it matters: Students learn that inquiry isn't about answering questions on a worksheet — it's about understanding the world and contributing to communities.

Standards activated: Knowledge Constructor, Global Collaborator, Creative Communicator, Empowered Learner, Digital Citizen.

Shift 2 – Think It Through, Test It, Improve It
Big Idea:

Students externalize their thinking, apply computational habits of mind, and refine their ideas, explanations, or solutions through iteration.

Small / Embedded Moves
  • Ask: "How did you arrive at that answer/idea?"
  • Ask: "What would happen if you tested that?"
  • Ask: "What changed or improved when you tried again?"
  • Model thinking-aloud, diagrams, sketches, or written drafts.
Classroom Routines

Draft → Feedback → Revise Loops

  • Students post rough drafts (text, video, code).
  • Peer or teacher feedback via structured protocol ("One strength, one question, one suggestion").
  • Revision reflection: What did I change and why?

Computational Thinking Routines

  • Decompose the problem: "Break it into smaller pieces."
  • Pattern recognition: "Where have I seen this before?"
  • Algorithm design: "Step-by-step, what must happen?"
  • Debugging: "Where's the error? How can I test?"

"Show Your Work" Protocols

  • Visual journals: diagrams, flowcharts, sketches.
  • Explain-back routines.
  • "Thinking log" – brief reflections on problem-solving steps.
Expanded / Extended Approaches
  • Design Thinking Projects:
    • Empathy → Define → Ideate → Prototype → Test cycles.
    • Students repeat the cycle to refine solutions.
  • Coding & Game Design Units: Computational thinking emerges naturally through debugging, playtesting, and iterating.
  • Argument/Essay Iteration: Students externalize reasoning through claims, evidence, and drafts, then revise based on feedback.

Why it matters: Students develop systematic approaches to problem-solving and learn resilience through iteration — essential for STEM and beyond.

Standards activated: Computational Thinker, Innovative Designer, Empowered Learner, Creative Communicator, Knowledge Constructor.

Shift 3 – Work Like a Team (Even When Apart)
Big Idea:

Students experience and reflect on real collaboration—both synchronous and asynchronous, local and global—and they learn the skills and mindsets that power team-based work.

Small / Embedded Moves
  • Ask: "What does each person on your team need to know?"
  • Ask: "How will you coordinate?"
  • Quick stand-up style check-ins:
    • "Who is doing what?"
    • "What's one thing we need to clarify?"
Classroom Routines

Collaborative Roles

  • Facilitator
  • Recorder
  • Evidence Finder
  • Summarizer
  • Connector (team-to-team or class-to-class liaison)

Collaboration Check Reflection

  • What helped us work well?
  • Where did we get stuck?
  • What will we change next time?

Asynchronous Workflows

  • Shared Google Slides/Docs
  • Flip video updates
  • Padlet or Miro boards
  • Commenting protocol ("TAG" – Tell something you like, Ask a question, Give a suggestion)
Expanded / Extended Approaches
  • Distributed Team Projects:
    • Kick-off meeting (synchronous).
    • Shared workspace (asynchronous).
    • Midpoint feedback videos.
    • Final live presentation or expo.
  • Global Collaboration Projects:
    • Partner with classes in other towns, states, or countries.
    • Exchange community stories, data sets, or designs.
    • Compare perspectives and revise jointly.
  • Global Café Model:
    • Clear structure: synchronous → asynchronous → synchronous.
    • Students co-produce artifacts to share.

Why it matters: Students learn how to function as collaborators in modern, distributed, team-based environments — the reality of the workplace and civic life.

Standards activated: Global Collaborator, Creative Communicator, Empowered Learner, Digital Citizen.

Shift 4 – Own Your Learning, Your Tools, and Your Processes
Big Idea:

Students become reflective, empowered thinkers who understand how they learn, why they choose tools, and which processes (inquiry, research, design, collaboration) best support them.

Small / Embedded Moves
  • Learning: "What did you learn about yourself today?"
  • Tools: "Why this tool?" "What do you need to learn about it?"
  • Processes: "Which step helped you most — research, drafting, revising, collaborating?"
Classroom Routines

Purpose → Process → Tool → Reflection Cycle

  1. Purpose: What are we trying to do?
  2. Process: Which strategy/steps best support that?
  3. Tool Choice: Which tool fits the task and audience?
  4. Reflection: What worked? What didn't? What do I change next time?

Tool/Process Swim Lanes

  • Students categorize tools and processes by purpose: inquiry, research, collaboration, design, communication, reflection.

Mini Skill-Building Moments

  • Students learn how to learn new tools: watching tutorials, collaborating to teach each other, using help menus, etc.
Expanded / Extended Approaches
  • Student Tool & Process Playbooks:
    • "How we research."
    • "How we revise."
    • "How we collaborate."
    • "How we choose tools."
  • Learning Journals:
    • Students track what tool or process choices they made and why.
  • Cross-Class Process Comparisons:
    • Partner classes share how they use tools differently, or how they structure research or revision.

Why it matters: Students become self-directed learners who can navigate changing tools, workflows, and expectations — essential for future learning and work.

Standards activated: Empowered Learner, Knowledge Constructor, Digital Citizen, Innovative Designer, Creative Communicator, Global Collaborator.

Shift 5 – Live the Digital Rules of the Road
Big Idea:

Digital citizenship becomes the everyday operating system for communication, collaboration, and creation — not a stand-alone lesson.

Small / Embedded Moves
  • Digital Pulse Check: Safe? Fair? Kind? Clear? Accurate?
  • Quick reminders about tone, privacy, or crediting sources.
Classroom Routines

Posting/Sharing Protocols

  • Reference sources
  • Respectful disagreement
  • Audience-appropriate tone
  • "Pause before posting" moments

Tool & Platform Norms

  • What's appropriate?
  • What never gets shared?
  • How do we credit?

Audience Awareness Moments

  • "How would you phrase this for a partner class?"
  • "How does this change when addressing a community audience?"
Expanded / Extended Approaches
  • Co-created norms with partner classes: Students negotiate shared expectations for communication and collaboration.
  • Student moderation of shared spaces: Students practice managing discussion boards, shared documents, or comment threads.
  • Digital Portfolio Projects: Students curate responsible and well-communicated work for authentic audiences.

Why it matters: Students learn to behave ethically and responsibly in digital spaces — essential for global collaboration and civic life.

Standards activated: Digital Citizen, Global Collaborator, Creative Communicator, Empowered Learner.
