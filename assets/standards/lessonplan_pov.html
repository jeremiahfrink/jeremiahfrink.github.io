Lesson 5 (Middle School Version)

Listening to Multiple Viewpoints

A Studio Experience in Empathy, Perspective-Taking, and Responsible AI Support
⏱ 2 hours — split over 2–3 shorter sessions

Lesson Overview

Focus

Students learn how to listen to, compare, and understand multiple viewpoints on the same situation. They explore how human interpretation includes nuance, emotion, and lived experience—while AI-generated summaries may be helpful but also incomplete. Students use AI only as a thought partner to compare interpretations and identify missing or flattened voices.

Why This Matters for Middle School

Middle schoolers regularly navigate conflicting opinions, misunderstandings, and issues of fairness. This lesson gives them structured, safe ways to:

  • listen deeply
  • notice perspective differences
  • identify whose voice is missing
  • think critically about AI summaries
  • develop empathy for a range of lived experiences

ISTE / Ohio Standards Alignment

Primary:

  • 1.7.b Multiple Viewpoints — Students consider diverse perspectives and compare interpretations of the same issue or interview.

Secondary:

  • 1.1 Empowered Learner
  • 1.3 Knowledge Constructor
  • 1.6 Creative Communicator
  • 1.2 Digital Citizen

Learning Goals

Students will be able to:

  • Conduct or analyze short interviews to surface lived experiences
  • Compare how different people experience the same event or issue
  • Use AI to generate summaries and identify where it misses nuance
  • Explain how listening to multiple perspectives improves understanding
  • Reflect on how AI can help but cannot replace human empathy

Studio Flow

SPARK

Purpose

Students discover how misunderstandings and assumptions arise when we only hear one version of a story.

Activity

  1. Starter Prompt: "What's something people often misunderstand about your opinion or experience?"
  2. Mini-Case Video or Scenario (5 min): Choose a short video with two different perspectives on the same small issue (ex: cafeteria problem, hallway congestion, playground conflict, homework expectations).

    Ask:

    • Who talked in this video?
    • Whose voice did we not hear?
    • How might someone else see this differently?
  3. Missing Voices Quick Chart: Students list:
    • Who spoke
    • Who didn't
    • Whose viewpoint might drastically change the story
  4. Pairs Brainstorm: Students develop 3–4 perspective-seeking questions, such as:
    • What's something important that others miss?
    • How does this situation affect you?
    • What happened from your point of view?

Optional AI Use OPTIONAL

Ask AI: "Give us 5 questions that help us understand multiple viewpoints on an issue." Students highlight which feel useful and which feel generic.

Checkpoint: Students have 2–3 strong viewpoint-seeking questions and can identify missing voices.

EXPLORE

Purpose

Students gather real human viewpoints on the same small, everyday issue. Depending on time, they can either conduct short interviews or work with pre-written student voice excerpts.

Activity

  1. Triad Roles:
    • Interviewer
    • Interviewee
    • Observer
  2. Interview Topic Options (student choice):
    • A time something felt unfair
    • A challenge at school (noise, transitions, homework)
    • Working with partners
    • A rule or expectation
    • Technology use or social media pressure
  3. Students conduct 1–2 short interviews using their perspective questions.
  4. Observer identifies:
    • Where the interviewee felt heard
    • Where the interviewer showed curiosity
    • Where another viewpoint might change the picture
  5. Group Synthesis: Students collect:
    • 3–5 quotes or paraphrases
    • 1 surprising viewpoint
    • 1 viewpoint that is still missing
Checkpoint: Each group has human-generated notes representing multiple viewpoints on one issue.

EXPERIMENT

Purpose

Students compare human interpretations to AI summaries to notice patterns, missing voices, and flattened nuance.

Activity

  1. Human First: Students cluster their quotes into themes:
    • Feelings or needs
    • Challenges
    • What they wish others understood
    • What viewpoints differ
  2. AI Baseline: Students anonymize notes and ask AI: "Summarize the top 2–3 viewpoints in these notes."
  3. AI With Guidance: Students try: "Summarize the viewpoints. Notice differences between people. Include nuance and uncertainty."
  4. Three-Column Comparison: Students fill in a chart:
    Human Viewpoints AI Summary AI Summary With "Multiple Viewpoints" Prompt
    Students fill this in during the activity

    Students mark with:

    • Circles: alignment
    • Stars: what humans noticed that AI missed
    • Underlines: moments AI flattened viewpoints into generalizations
  5. Annotate the Gaps: Students answer:
    • Which human voices did AI miss?
    • Where did AI combine different viewpoints?
    • What subtle emotions or contradictions didn't appear?
Checkpoint: Students can explain how AI summaries differ from human interpretation and why multiple viewpoints matter.

CREATE

Purpose

Students create a Multiple Viewpoints Insight Map—a simple, shareable artifact that highlights viewpoint differences and why they matter.

Components (students build all four):

  1. The Issue Statement: "One issue we explored was... from the perspective of..."
  2. 3–4 Key Viewpoints (each with a quote or phrase):
    • Viewpoint A (why this matters to them)
    • Viewpoint B (how their experience differs)
    • Viewpoint C (what nuance was surprising)
    • Missing Viewpoint (whose voice is still not represented)
  3. AI Gap Notes:
    • One thing AI got right
    • One thing AI oversimplified
    • One thing AI completely missed
  4. Why Multiple Viewpoints Matter:
    • How hearing diverse viewpoints changes understanding
    • How it could prevent misunderstandings
    • How it can support better decisions or fairness

Optional Digital Tooling OPTIONAL

Students may build their maps using a slide, Jamboard, Canva, or notebook page.

Checkpoint: Students produce a clear, accessible artifact showing comparison of multiple viewpoints and AI's limitations.

SHARE

Purpose

Students present their Multiple Viewpoints Insight Maps and learn how others interpreted the same issue differently.

Structure

  1. Gallery Walk or Pair Shares: Feedback prompts:
    • "I learned a viewpoint I hadn't considered..."
    • "AI missed something important when..."
    • "If we only heard one viewpoint, we might have believed..."
  2. Small-Group Discussion:
    • "How do misunderstandings happen when only one viewpoint is heard?"
    • "How would a teacher, parent, or principal see this differently?"
  3. Whole-Class Synthesis: What categories of viewpoints showed up? Where did students disagree?
Checkpoint: Students articulate the value of holding multiple viewpoints.

REFLECT

Purpose

Students reflect on listening, interpreting, and using AI responsibly.

Prompts (Choose 2–3):

  • What new viewpoint surprised you today?
  • How did comparing viewpoints change your understanding of the issue?
  • Where did AI help?
  • Where did AI flatten or combine viewpoints unfairly?
  • Why is perspective-taking important for fairness and community?
  • How will you listen differently next time?

Option: Quick-Write or Audio Note OPTIONAL

Checkpoint: Students show understanding of both empathy and the importance of multiple viewpoints in decision-making.

Optional Middle School Extensions

  • Use a global partner class to gather two contrasting viewpoints on the same prompt.
  • Analyze two conflicting news articles and identify differing perspectives.
  • Use AI to rewrite a viewpoint in different tones and evaluate its accuracy.
