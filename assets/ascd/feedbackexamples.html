● Rethinking Assessment in a Modern Age

Subject-Specific Feedback Moves

This page offers examples of how different subject areas can strengthen feedback practice in Section 2: Feedback in Today’s Classroom. Each subject shows a key feedback move and one concrete way to build it into daily assessment.

Mathematics
Respond to student strategies, not just answers.
›

In math, high-quality feedback highlights how students are thinking—what strategies they use, where their reasoning is strong, and where a small shift could unlock understanding.

Focus feedback on strategy and reasoning
Example

Instead of: “Wrong, check your work.”
Use: “I notice you used repeated addition here. How might a number line or array help you see the groups more clearly?”

Use micro-feedback during work time
Example

During math workshop, leave quick sticky-note comments like: “Try drawing a picture before you choose an operation.” or “You’ve got the right operation—now estimate first to see if your answer makes sense.”

Build student-led error analysis
Example

After a quiz, project one or two anonymous “favorite mistakes” and ask: “What do you think this student was trying to do? Where did the math go off track?” Have students write one sentence of feedback to the “student.”

English Language Arts
Guide stronger thinking, not just cleaner writing.
›

In ELA, feedback can help students strengthen claims, evidence, reasoning, and voice. It works best when it’s specific and tied to criteria they understand.

Target claims, evidence, and explanation
Example

Comment on an essay with: “Your claim is clear. Now choose one quote that best supports it and add 2–3 sentences explaining why this quote matters.” Instead of just “Add more detail.”

Use a quick draft–feedback–revise cycle
Example

Have students submit only their thesis and one body paragraph. Give focused feedback on those pieces, then have students revise them before completing the full essay.

Anchor feedback with exemplars
Example

Share two sample paragraphs (one emerging, one strong) and ask: “Which is closer to your current writing? What’s one move from the strong example you could try in your next draft?”

Science
Address misconceptions and strengthen explanations.
›

In science, feedback should surface and correct misconceptions, focus on how students connect evidence to ideas, and encourage revision of models and explanations.

Name and nudge misconceptions
Example

Instead of: “This is wrong.”
Use: “It sounds like you’re thinking heat is a substance that ‘moves into’ objects. How might we describe it instead using particle motion?”

Use guiding questions tied to phenomena
Example

On an explanation of a phenomenon, respond with: “What evidence from your investigation supports this idea?” or “How does your model show what happens to particles as temperature changes?”

Encourage model and explanation revision
Example

After initial models, ask students to revise with a prompt: “Update your model to show what you learned from the lab. Write 1–2 sentences explaining what you changed and why.”

Social Studies
Push deeper sourcing and reasoning.
›

In social studies, feedback can strengthen how students use sources, build arguments, recognize bias, and connect past and present.

Focus feedback on sourcing and evidence
Example

On a paragraph citing a source, write: “You used this source for your claim. Why is this a good source for this question? What makes it reliable or relevant?”

Prompt deeper reasoning about claims
Example

When a student makes a claim, respond with: “You’ve stated your opinion. Add one piece of historical evidence that supports it and explain how they connect.”

Invite perspective-taking in feedback
Example

On a student’s interpretation of an event, ask: “How might someone from a different group at the time have viewed this event? Add one sentence from that perspective.”

World Languages
Support communication clarity and confidence.
›

In world languages, feedback works best when it focuses on helping students communicate more clearly and confidently, rather than simply correcting every error.

Prioritize meaning over perfection
Example

On a spoken response, say: “Your meaning is clear and your pace is good. Let’s work on this one sound/structure next time: …” instead of correcting every small mistake.

Use audio/video feedback for pronunciation
Example

Record a short response back to the student modeling correct pronunciation of a few tricky words they used, and invite them to repeat and re-record.

Offer sentence starters and rephrasing prompts
Example

On a written task, instead of “Add more detail,” give 1–2 sentence stems in the target language that students can use to extend their ideas.

Specials (Art, Music, PE, Tech, STEM, etc.)
Give immediate, actionable, and growth-oriented feedback.
›

In specials, feedback is often most powerful when it is timely, specific, and focused on helping students refine their technique, creativity, and performance over time.

Give immediate, bite-sized feedback during practice
Example

In music, while students rehearse, use quick comments like: “Try that phrase again, but focus only on your articulation.” or “Your rhythm is strong—now listen for your tone on the longer notes.”

Highlight growth over time
Example

In art, respond to a portfolio with: “Compare your first and most recent piece. What’s one technique that has clearly improved? I notice…” and add a specific observation.

Use simple rubrics that balance skill and creativity
Example

In a tech/STEM project, give feedback on two dimensions: “Functionality (Does it work?)” and “Design/Creativity (How user-friendly or original is it?)”, and name one strength and one next step in each.
