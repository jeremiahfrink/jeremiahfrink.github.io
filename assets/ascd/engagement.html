Planning with Purpose

Engagement is designed: Do • Feel • Think

Engagement isn’t compliance and it isn’t “flashy.” It’s lesson design that supports what students do (behavioral engagement), how they feel (emotional engagement), and how deeply they think (cognitive engagement). Disengagement can look different in different students—so we need a set of purposeful design moves.

1
Build Ownership with Micro-Agency + Feedback Loops
“Mission Briefing → Challenge Zone → Save Point”
⌄

Core strategy

Design lessons with (a) a clear entry (“mission”), (b) choice + feedback during work time, and (c) a short reflection checkpoint to lock in learning and next steps.

Engagement connection

  • Behavioral: predictable routines for participation + revision (students know what “doing the work” looks like).
  • Emotional: identity/belonging rises when students “see themselves in the work.”
  • Cognitive: reflection + feedback tightens thinking (metacognition and self-regulation).

TLPs

Agency + Reflection (and often Equity via multiple pathways).

Disengagement profiles it hits

  • Behavioral disengagement (“Disruptor”): structure + clear moves reduce drift.
  • Cognitive disengagement (“Compliant”): reflection/checkpoints prevent “doing school” without thinking.

2
Hook Relevance with an “Inquiry-to-Impact” Launch
Anchor → Question → Create → Teach Forward
⌄

Core strategy

Start with an authentic anchor, generate/sort student questions, then move toward making/sharing something that helps others (“teach it forward”).

Engagement connection

  • Emotional: the “anchor in the real world” creates relevance and belonging (“this matters here”).
  • Cognitive: questioning + source evaluation drives real thinking (not just completion).
  • Behavioral: students have clear actions (questioning, curating, producing, sharing).

TLPs

Authenticity + Inquiry + Reflection.

Disengagement profiles it hits

  • Emotionally disengaged (“Alienated”): relevance + voice reduces disconnection.
  • Cognitively disengaged (“Compliant”): the question-driven arc demands meaning-making, not just “right answers.”
Primary: Emotional Primary: Cognitive Supports: Behavioral
3
Move from “Assignment” to “Meaningful Challenge” through Remix + Redesign
Reveal → Reframe → Redesign → Reflect
⌄

Core strategy

Take something you already teach and “remix” it so students make real decisions, design for an audience, and revise based on feedback.

Engagement connection

  • Cognitive: redesign requires synthesis and transfer (students must transform ideas).
  • Emotional: creativity + audience increases pride/connection.
  • Behavioral: structured cycle keeps work moving and visible.

TLPs

Agency + Authenticity + Reflection (and often Equity via multiple voices/choices).

Disengagement profiles it hits

  • Cognitive disengagement (“Compliant”): shifts from “for a grade” toward purpose.
  • Behavioral disengagement (“Disruptor”): creative production + roles can reduce off-task behavior by giving meaningful ownership.
Primary: Cognitive Primary: Emotional Supports: Behavioral
4
Design for Deep Thinking with a Problem-Solving Routine
Puzzle Breakdown + Iteration
⌄

Core strategy

Use a repeatable “puzzle” routine that normalizes persistence, strategy, and iteration—so challenge becomes a feature, not a bug.

Engagement connection

  • Cognitive: students must plan, test, revise (real thinking).
  • Behavioral: routines support stamina and on-task behaviors during challenge.
  • Emotional: builds confidence/efficacy (“I can do hard things”).

TLPs

Inquiry (as problem investigation) + Reflection (after attempts) + Agency (strategy selection).

Disengagement profiles it hits

  • Cognitive disengagement (“Compliant”): makes learning visible through reasoning, not just answers.
  • Behavioral disengagement (“Disruptor”): engaging, structured challenge can channel energy into the task.
Primary: Cognitive Supports: Behavioral Supports: Emotional
5
Create Belonging + Accountability with Structured Collaboration
Not “group work” → true collaboration
⌄

Core strategy

Treat collaboration as a designed experience: roles, norms, empathy, and structures for sharing/challenging ideas (so everyone has a seat at the table and ideas are shared, challenged, and refined).

Engagement connection

  • Emotional: belonging + supportive relationships (directly aligned to the “focus on relationships” takeaway).
  • Behavioral: clear participation expectations and shared responsibility.
  • Cognitive: students refine thinking through dialogue and multiple viewpoints.

TLPs

Collaboration + Equity (voice) + Reflection (team debrief).

Disengagement profiles it hits

  • Emotionally disengaged (“Alienated”): connection + being needed by a team.
  • Behaviorally disengaged (“Disruptor”): clear roles/norms can reduce conflict and increase participation.
Primary: Emotional Primary: Behavioral Supports: Cognitive
