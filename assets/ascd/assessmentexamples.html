● Rethinking Assessment in a Modern Age

Subject-Specific Assessment Moves

This page offers examples of how different subject areas can strengthen assessment design in Section 1: What Makes a Good Assessment? Each subject shows a key consideration and one concrete way to build it into classroom practice.

Mathematics
Highlight reasoning, not just right answers.
›

In math, strong assessment tasks make student thinking visible. They go beyond “Can you get the answer?” to “Can you explain, justify, and choose a strategy?”

Assess reasoning, not just procedure
Example

Instead of giving 10 fraction problems to solve, ask: “Solve two of these problems and explain why your approach works. Then describe a common mistake someone might make.”

Use math talks or explanation-based tasks
Example

Have students record a 1–2 minute audio or video explaining how they solved a problem and why they chose that strategy. Assess both correctness and clarity of reasoning.

Provide multiple representations
Example

Let students show their thinking using a number line, array model, table, or equation. The assessment prompt might say: “Show and explain your solution using at least one model of your choice.”

Move beyond single-answer formats
Example

Turn a traditional question into a “many correct answers” task: “Create two different sets of numbers that both have a mean of 12. Explain how you know the mean is 12 each time.”

English Language Arts
Value process, voice, and interpretation.
›

In ELA, good assessment captures how students interpret, support ideas with evidence, and develop their voice— not just whether they can produce a polished final piece.

Balance product with process
Example

When assessing a literary essay, include a planning sheet or annotated passage as part of the submission. Grade both the final essay and the evidence of how they built it.

Assess interpretation and evidence, not summary
Example

Replace “Describe the theme of the story” with: “Choose one quote that best represents the theme and explain why it matters for the character or plot.”

Avoid overly generic, AI-friendly prompts
Example

Instead of: “Analyze how the main character changes.”
Use: “Where in the book did you first notice the character beginning to change? What made you notice it?” This roots the response in specific reading moments.

Include reflection and personal connection
Example

After an essay, add a short reflection prompt: “What did you personally connect with in this character’s experience, and how did that shape your interpretation?”

Science
Emphasize explanation, modeling, and phenomena.
›

In science, effective assessment goes beyond recall. It asks students to explain, model, and reason about phenomena using scientific ideas and evidence.

Assess concepts, not just facts
Example

Rather than “Label the parts of the cell,” ask: “What would happen if the mitochondria stopped working? Explain your reasoning.”

Use modeling and explanations
Example

Ask students to create a simple model (drawing or digital) of a phenomenon like sound traveling through air, then record or write an explanation of how their model shows what is happening.

Highlight inquiry decisions, not just lab results
Example

Add a question to lab reports: “What was the most important decision you made while designing or running your investigation, and why?”

Connect to real-world phenomena
Example

Use prompts such as: “You see condensation forming on a cold drink outside. Explain why this happens using the water cycle.”

Social Studies
Center sourcing, argument, and perspective.
›

In social studies, good assessment focuses on how students work with sources, construct arguments, and understand perspectives—not just how many facts they can recall.

Assess argumentation and sourcing
Example

Include a sourcing task such as: “Which of these two sources is more reliable for understanding this event, and why?”

Blend primary and secondary sources
Example

Ask students to choose one primary and one secondary source and explain how each adds to their understanding of a historical question.

Elicit stance and reasoning, not just summary
Example

Change “Explain the causes of the American Revolution” to: “Which cause of the American Revolution do you think had the biggest impact, and why? Support your view with evidence.”

Connect history to present-day issues
Example

Use prompts like: “Choose a present-day issue and explain one way it connects to a historical pattern we studied this unit.”

World Languages
Prioritize authentic communication.
›

In world languages, meaningful assessment focuses on real communication—speaking, listening, reading, and writing in ways that approximate authentic use of the language.

Assess speaking and listening, not just written work
Example

Have students record a 1-minute spoken response to a prompt, and assess pronunciation, fluency, and clarity of meaning.

Use real-life communicative tasks
Example

Assessment task: “You’re at a café. Record yourself ordering and asking 3 questions (about ingredients, price, or recommendations), then respond naturally.”

Reveal comprehension, not just translation skills
Example

Play a short audio clip, then ask students to identify the main idea and one supporting detail, either in the target language or in English.

Include spontaneous speaking
Example

Show a picture (e.g., a messy room or busy street) and have students describe what they see in the target language in a quick, 10–20 second recording.

Specials (Art, Music, PE, Tech, STEM, etc.)
Value process, practice, and creative choices.
›

In specials, assessment can spotlight growth, creativity, and skill development over time. The goal is to capture how students practice, revise, and make choices—not just the final performance or product.

Assess process along with product
Example

In art, ask students to submit two drafts of a piece plus a short note describing what changed based on feedback or new ideas.

Use multiple forms of evidence
Example

In PE, have students record a quick video of a skill (like a jump shot or overhand throw) and label what they did well and what they want to improve.

Highlight practice and improvement
Example

In music, ask students to record the same short passage twice a week apart and write two sentences about how their tone or rhythm improved.

Include self-assessment and reflection
Example

In any creative subject, add: “Circle the part of your project where you made your strongest artistic or design choice, and explain why.”
