● Rethinking Assessment in a Modern Age

Interactive Assessment Redesign Guide

Use this page to walk through your own assessment task, step by step. Each section includes a short framing and reflective questions. Work with the same task all the way through to see how it evolves.

Step 0: Choose Your Assessment Task

Before you begin, pick one existing assessment or learning task you already use (quiz, project, writing prompt, lab, performance task, etc.). You will revisit this same task in every section.

  • Core classroom task
  • Real students, real context
  • Use it all the way through

Tip: Pick something you’d actually be willing to adjust in the next few weeks.

Section 1
What Makes This a Good Assessment?
Clarify purpose, type, and quality of your current task.
›

Start by naming what your task is really trying to measure and how well it does that job. This gives you a baseline before you add feedback, technology, or AI.

Questions to ask yourself
  • What is this task trying to assess? Write the learning goal in one clear sentence.
  • Is this primarily assessment for learning, as learning, or of learning?
  • What type of assessment is it (quick check, quiz, project, performance task, portfolio entry, etc.)?
  • What evidence of student thinking does this task actually give you?
  • Does the task allow for different ways students could show understanding, or is it very narrow?
  • Is the task equitable and accessible for the range of learners in your classroom?

Try to be descriptive, not judgmental. You’re just surfacing what the assessment currently is and does.

Section 2
Feedback: How Are You Responding to Student Learning?
Examine the feedback patterns around this task.
›

Feedback is where assessment turns into growth. Look at how, when, and why students currently receive feedback on this task.

Questions to ask yourself
  • How do students currently receive feedback on this task (comments, scores, conferences, rubrics, etc.)?
  • When does that feedback typically arrive—during the work, after submission, or both?
  • Would a student be able to tell, from your feedback, where they are now and what to do next?
  • How often is your feedback specific and actionable versus general (“good job,” “needs work,” etc.)?
  • Do students have a chance to revise or respond to your feedback, or is it one-and-done?
  • How do you help students interpret and use feedback (not just receive it)?

Optional: Imagine one student response to this task and draft one sentence of feedback on a strength and one on a next step.

Section 3
Technology-Supported Evidence & Feedback
Expand how students show learning and how you respond.
›

Consider how technology could broaden the types of evidence you collect and make feedback cycles more manageable and student-centered.

Questions to ask yourself
  • For this same task, what is one additional way students could demonstrate understanding (video, audio, screencast, image, simulation, etc.)?
  • What is one tool that could help you capture that kind of evidence?
  • How might technology help you give timelier or more specific feedback (e.g., audio comments, comment banks, rubric tools)?
  • Could students use a tool (portfolios, reflection forms, goal trackers) to own and organize their evidence over time?
  • What’s one barrier (time, access, complexity) that technology might actually reduce for you or your students?
  • Where do you need to keep things intentionally low-tech to focus on thinking and relationships?

You don’t need to adopt every tool—identify one or two high-leverage tweaks that make the task more meaningful and manageable.

Section 4
AI & Modern Tools: Stress-Testing Your Task
Ask, “What happens if AI does this assignment?”
›

Imagine (or actually test) how an AI tool might complete your task. Use that insight to see where your assessment still reveals student thinking—and where it needs redesign.

Questions to ask yourself
  • If you asked an AI tool to complete this task, which parts would it handle easily?
  • Which aspects of the task would still require student-specific thinking (personal experience, context, reasoning, making choices)?
  • Does the task currently capture the student’s process, or only the final product?
  • What new evidence could you collect (think-alouds, drafts, reflections, process logs) to make student thinking visible?
  • What validity questions does AI raise for this task (Who did this? How do I know? Is this aligned with our goals?)?
  • How might you explicitly invite or constrain AI use so that it supports learning instead of replacing it?

The goal isn’t to “catch” AI use; it’s to design tasks where using AI still requires—and highlights—student understanding.

Section 5
AI as Your Co-Designer (and Feedback Assistant)
Use AI to support your work, not define it.
›

Now, think about where AI could help you design or refine this assessment and its feedback loops, while you still provide the professional judgment and conceptual guidance.

Questions to ask yourself
  • For this task, where could AI help you draft something (rubric, success criteria, exemplars, modified versions, feedback comments)?
  • What would you need to edit or adjust in AI-generated suggestions to make them fit your students and context?
  • Could AI suggest scaffolds or extension options for different readiness levels in your class?
  • Where might AI help you summarize student work or patterns so you can focus your time on deeper feedback?
  • How can you keep yourself as the one responsible for conceptual accuracy and fairness, even when using AI?
  • What expectations will you set with students about when and how they can use AI on this task?

Think of AI as a drafting partner and assistant—not as the assessor. You decide what to keep, adapt, or ignore.

Section 6
Bringing It Together: Quick Redesign
Make one meaningful change you could try soon.
›

Synthesize your thinking by making a small but concrete adjustment to this task. Focus on what will make assessment more meaningful, responsive, and manageable.

Questions to ask yourself
  • Looking back at your answers, what is one part of this task you want to redesign first?
  • Will you change the task itself, the evidence you collect, the feedback process, the use of technology, or how you acknowledge AI use?
  • How would you describe your change in 1–2 clear sentences to a colleague?
  • What will students notice is different about this assessment and how it’s used?
  • What is a realistic next step and timeline for trying this change?
  • How will you know if the redesign made assessment more meaningful for students and more informative for you?

Capture your final idea as a brief “assessment redesign statement” you can bring back to your team or try in your next unit.
