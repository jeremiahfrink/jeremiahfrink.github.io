High-Leverage Moves

Where to Focus Your Energy

Research and practice suggest that Challenging Problem/Question, Authenticity, and Student Voice & Choice often have the biggest ripple effect—strengthening these tends to lift other elements. Select 2-3 elements to focus on below.

Challenging Problem or Question

Guiding Questions
  • Is there a real problem students are working to solve, or a genuine question they're trying to answer?
  • Is it open-ended enough that there isn't one "right" answer?
  • Is it challenging enough to require sustained effort and deep thinking?
  • Would someone outside of school actually care about this question?

Authenticity

Guiding Questions
  • Who outside of school actually cares about this topic or problem?
  • Are students using tools, processes, or methods that professionals use?
  • Could students' work actually matter to someone beyond the teacher?
  • Does this feel "real" or does it feel like a school exercise dressed up?

Student Voice & Choice

Guiding Questions
  • What meaningful decisions do students get to make about their work?
  • Can students pursue aspects of the topic that genuinely interest them?
  • Do students have choice in how they demonstrate their learning?
  • Are students contributing to defining the problem, questions, or direction—or just following your plan?

Sustained Inquiry

Guiding Questions
  • Does the project require students to ask questions, find resources, and apply information over time?
  • Are students generating new questions as they learn, not just answering yours?
  • Is there enough complexity that students can't just "Google the answer"?
  • Does the timeline allow for depth, or is it a rush to a product?

Reflection

Guiding Questions
  • When and how do students pause to think about what they're learning?
  • Do students reflect on both the content AND their process/collaboration?
  • Is reflection built in throughout, or just tacked on at the end?
  • Does reflection actually influence what students do next?

Critique & Revision

Guiding Questions
  • Are students taught how to give specific, helpful feedback?
  • Do students have structured opportunities to give AND receive feedback?
  • Is there time and expectation built in for students to actually revise based on feedback?
  • Does feedback come from multiple sources (peers, teacher, outside experts)?

Public Product

Guiding Questions
  • Who will see students' final work beyond you?
  • Is the audience authentic—people who actually care about the topic?
  • Does having a public audience raise the stakes and quality of work?
  • Could the product take a form that's actually used in the real world?

Challenging Problem or Question

Starter Prompt
I'm designing a PBL unit about [your topic]. Right now, my driving question is [your current question or "I don't have one yet"]. Help me develop a more compelling driving question by asking me questions about: who actually deals with this issue in the real world, what tensions or debates exist around this topic, and what would make students genuinely curious rather than just compliant.
Follow-up Prompt
Now that we have some question options, help me pressure-test them: Which of these questions is open-ended enough that students couldn't just Google the answer? Which would sustain several weeks of inquiry? Which might students actually argue about or have different perspectives on?

Authenticity

Starter Prompt
I'm working on a unit about [your topic] for [grade level] students. I want to make it feel more real and less like "school." Interview me about my unit and then help me identify: What professionals or community members actually work on problems like this? What authentic formats could student work take? What real audiences might genuinely care about student findings or solutions?
Follow-up Prompt
Given what we've discussed, what's the difference between my students "playing school" with this topic versus actually doing something real? Be honest with me—where does my current plan still feel artificial, and what would it take to close that gap?

Student Voice & Choice

Starter Prompt
In my unit about [your topic], students currently [describe what students do]. I want to give students more meaningful choice without losing focus on the learning goals. Ask me about my learning objectives, my constraints (time, resources, requirements), and my students—then help me identify 2-3 specific decision points where students could have real agency.
Follow-up Prompt
I'm worried that giving students too much choice will mean some miss important content or skills. Help me think through: What's the difference between "choice in how to learn" versus "choice in what to learn"? Where are the non-negotiables in my unit, and where is there genuine flexibility I haven't tapped into?

Sustained Inquiry

Starter Prompt
My unit on [topic] currently runs for [timeframe]. I want to make sure the inquiry is genuinely sustained and rigorous, not just busy work spread over time. Walk me through: What makes a question worth multiple weeks of investigation? How do I build in complexity that unfolds over time rather than front-loading all the information?
Follow-up Prompt
Help me map out what genuine inquiry could look like week-by-week. Where should new questions emerge? When should students hit productive struggle? How do I keep the momentum without just telling them answers when they get stuck?

Reflection

Starter Prompt
I want to build meaningful reflection into my PBL unit on [topic], but I find that reflection often feels forced or students just go through the motions. Help me design reflection that actually changes what students do next. Ask me about my project timeline and what decisions students will be making along the way.
Follow-up Prompt
Give me 3-4 specific reflection prompts or protocols I could use at different points in this project—ones that go beyond "what did you learn?" and actually help students think metacognitively about their process, collaboration, and growth.

Critique & Revision

Starter Prompt
In my unit, students will create [describe the product/output]. I want to build in meaningful critique and revision, but I'm not sure how to structure it so feedback is actually useful and students have time to implement it. Help me think through: When should critique happen? Who should give feedback? What does "good feedback" look like for this kind of work?
Follow-up Prompt
Design a critique protocol I could use with my students. I want something structured enough that students know how to participate, but flexible enough to surface genuine insights. Include how I'd introduce and model it, and how students would capture feedback to use later.

Public Product

Starter Prompt
My students are working on [describe project] and right now the only audience is me. Help me brainstorm authentic audiences who would actually care about this work—not just "be nice to kids" but genuinely find value in what students produce. Consider: local community, professionals in related fields, other students, online communities, organizations working on this issue.
Follow-up Prompt
Given the audience options we discussed, help me think through logistics: How would I actually connect students with this audience? What format would make the most sense? How do I prepare students to present to people outside of school? What could go wrong and how do I mitigate it?

Save your reflection to reference as you develop your unit.
