Judging Process & Methodology

The ISTE + ASCD AI Innovator Challenge 2026 received 118 submissions from student teams across 21 countries. Through a structured, multi-round evaluation process involving 42 judges, we identified 10 recommended finalists for Round 3, along with 10 alternates and 5 additional mentions.

1

Initial Screening

118 submissions → 113 qualified
▶

All 118 submissions were reviewed for completeness and accessibility. Five submissions were removed because they did not meet basic submission requirements:

  • Missing required artifacts (pitch video, digital product)
  • Broken or inaccessible links
  • Failure to respond to requests for corrected materials

The remaining 113 projects moved into Round 1 judging.

2

Round 1 — Ready to Share

113 projects × 2 judges each → 66 advanced
▶

Each of the 113 qualifying projects was assigned to 2 independent judges. Judges evaluated submissions across 7 rubric dimensions, each scored 1–4, for a maximum of 28:

  • Connection to Community Problem & SDG
  • Impact, Relevance & Longevity
  • Responsible & Ethical Use of AI
  • Innovation & Creativity
  • Design Quality & Accessibility
  • Collaboration & Process
  • Presentation & Communication

Judges also indicated whether they felt each submission should advance to Round 2.

Advancement criteria:

  • Minimum average score of 20 out of 28
  • No rubric dimension rated 1 by either judge
  • No dimension rated 2 by both judges
  • Both judges recommending advancement

We were intentionally generous with borderline cases when scoring discrepancies or a single harsh evaluator appeared to be the deciding factor.

66 projects advanced to Round 2, representing 15 countries.

3

Round 1 Review & Quality Checks

Scoring patterns, discrepancies & judge calibration
▶

Before advancing projects, we conducted a thorough review of Round 1 scoring:

  • Completion verification — Confirmed each project had been scored by its full set of assigned judges
  • Scoring discrepancies — Identified projects where judges differed by 6 or more points and investigated each case
  • Judge pattern analysis — Reviewed evaluators who were consistently scoring far above or below the field average (overall average: 21.7/28)
  • Criteria-level flags — Checked for any rubric dimension rated 1 by either judge, or rated 2 by both judges
  • Borderline review — Projects scoring between 18–21 were manually reviewed, especially where one harsh judge appeared to be the deciding factor
4

Round 2 — Website Highlights & Finalist Recommendation

66 projects × 3 judges each
▶

For Round 2, each of the 66 advancing projects was assigned to 3 independent judges — an increase from 2 in Round 1 to provide stronger signal for finalist decisions.

No judge who scored a project in Round 1 was assigned that same project in Round 2, ensuring fresh perspectives on every submission.

Judges used the same 7-dimension rubric and were additionally asked whether they would recommend each project as a finalist to represent ISTE at ISTELive 2026.

5

Round 2 Analysis

Consensus, gaps, cross-round patterns & judge calibration
▶

Following Round 2 scoring, we analyzed:

  • Judge consensus — Whether all 3 judges recommended finalist status
  • Scoring gaps — Disagreements of 6 or more points between judges on the same project
  • Cross-round shifts — Projects with significant score changes between Round 1 and Round 2, investigating whether shifts reflected judge calibration differences or genuine quality signals
  • Low-scorer investigation — Whether judges who scored a project notably lower were part of a broader pattern of harsh scoring, or representing a legitimate critical evaluation
  • Combined scoring — Weighted Round 2 at 60% and Round 1 at 40%, reflecting that Round 2 represents the most recent and focused evaluation
6

Finalist Selection Criteria

How the three tiers were determined
▶

Round 3 Finalists (10 projects)
R2 average ≥ 25/28 with unanimous recommendation from all 3 judges. One project was included with a R2 average just below 25 because it achieved unanimous recommendation — the only project outside the initial group with full consensus. The committee felt that unanimous agreement to advance carried significant weight.

Alternates (10 projects)
R2 average ≥ 25/28 with majority recommendation (2 of 3 judges). In several cases, the dissenting judge still scored the project 24–26 but set a higher personal bar for what constitutes a "finalist." These reflect selectivity in the evaluation, not concerns about project quality. These projects would be well-suited for recognition on the ISTE + ASCD website and could be considered if the committee wishes to expand the finalist pool.

Mentions (5 projects)
Combined weighted score ≥ 23 with majority recommendation, but R2 averages below 25. Quality work that performed well across both rounds and merits recognition.

✓

Process Integrity

Safeguards applied throughout both rounds
▶
  • Judge anonymity — Only anonymized judge codes were used throughout. Judge identities were kept separate from all shared portals and reports.
  • Fresh eyes for Round 2 — No judge evaluated the same project in both rounds, preventing anchoring bias from prior scores.
  • Scoring pattern monitoring — Judges whose scoring showed very low variance (e.g., scoring every project nearly identically) or extreme averages (consistently 5+ points above or below the field average) were flagged and their impact on specific projects was reviewed.
  • Discrepancy review — All projects with large scoring gaps between judges were individually examined to determine whether the gap reflected a genuine difference of opinion or a calibration issue with a specific evaluator.
